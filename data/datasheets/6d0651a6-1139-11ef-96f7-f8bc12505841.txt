A few metals form dens e, stable, tightly adhering,  \nelectrically insulating oxid es. These are the so-called  \n“valve”metals and include titanium, zirconium, niobium,  \ntantalum, hafnium, and aluminum. Only a few of these  \npermit the accurate contro l of oxide thickness by  \nelectrochemical means. Of these, the most valuable for the  \nelectronics industry are aluminum and tantalum.Capacitors are basic to all kinds of electrical equipment,  \nfrom radios and television sets to missile controls and  \nautomobile ignitions. Their function is to store an electrical  charge for later use.Capacitors consist of two co nducting surfaces, usually  \nmetal plates, whose function is to conduct electricity. They  \nare separated by an insulating material or dielectric. The  \ndielectric used in all tantalum electrolytic capacitors is  \ntantalum pentoxide.Tantalum pentoxide compound  possesses high-dielectric  \nstrength and a high-dielectric constant. As capacitors are  \nbeing manufactured, a film of tantalum pentox ide is applied  \nto their electrodes by means of  an electrolytic process. The  \nfilm is applied in various thic knesses and at various voltages  \nand although transparent to begin with, it takes on different  \ncolors as light refracts through it. This coloring occurs on the  \ntantalum electrodes of all types of tantalum capacitors.Rating for rating, tantalum capacitors tend to have as much  \nas three times better capacitance/volume efficiency than  \naluminum electrolytic capacitors.
