J | Page 9 of 14 CIRCUIT DESCRIPTION \nThe ADM6316/ADM6318/ADM6319/ADM6320/ADM6321/ \nADM6322 provide microprocessor supply voltage supervision by controlling the reset input of the microprocessor. Code execution errors are avoided during power-up, power-down, and brownout \nconditions by asserting a reset signal when the supply voltage is \nbelow a preset threshold and by allowing supply voltage stabiliza-tion with a fixed timeout reset pulse after the supply voltage rises above the threshold. In addition, problems with microprocessor code execution can be monitored and corrected with a watchdog \ntimer (ADM6316/ADM6318/ADM6320/ADM6321). If the user \ndetects a problem with the system’s operation, a manual reset input is available (ADM6316/ADM6319/ADM6320/ADM6322) to reset the microprocessor, for example, by means of an external push button. \nRESET OUTPUT \nThe ADM6316 features an active low push-pull reset output, while the ADM6321/ADM6322 have active high push-pull reset outputs. The ADM6318/ADM6319 feature dual active low and \nactive high push-pull reset outputs. For active low and active \nhigh outputs, the reset signal is guaranteed to be logic low and logic high, respectively, for V\nCC down to 1 V . \nThe reset output is asserted when V CC is below the reset thresh-\nold (V TH), when MR is driven low, or when WDI is not serviced \nwithin the watchdog timeout period (t WD). Reset remains asserted \nfor the duration of the reset active timeout period (t RP) after V CC \nrises above the reset threshold, after MR transitions from low to \nhigh, or after the watchdog timer times out. Reset Timing Diagram \nOPEN-DRAIN RESET  OUTPUT \nThe ADM6320/ADM6321/ADM6322 have an active low, open-\ndrain reset output. This output structure requires an external \npull-up resistor to connect the reset output to a voltage rail no \nhigher than 6 V . The resistor must comply with the micropro-cessor’s logic low and logic high voltage level requirements while \nsupplying input current and leakage paths on the RESET\n line. A \n10 kΩ resistor is adequate in most situations. MANUAL RESET INPUT \nThe ADM6316/ADM6319/ADM6320/ADM6322 feature a manual reset input ( MR\n), which when driven low, asserts the reset output. \nWhen MR transitions from low to high, reset remains asserted for \nthe duration of the reset active timeout period before deasserting. The MR\n input has a 52 kΩ, internal pull-up so that the input is \nalways high when unconnected. An external push-button switch can be connected between MR\n and ground so that the user can \ngenerate a reset. Debounce circuitry for this purpose is integrated on chip. Noise immunity is provided on the MR\n input, and fast, \nnegative going transients of up to 100 ns (typical) are ignored. A 0.1 μF capacitor between MR\n and ground provides additional \nnoise immunity. \nWATCHDOG INPUT \nThe ADM6316/ADM6318/ADM6320/ADM6321 feature a \nwatchdog timer that monitors microprocessor activity. A timer circuit is cleared with every low-to-high or high-to-low logic transition on the watchdog input pin (WDI), which detects pulses as short as 50 ns. If the timer counts through the preset watchdog \ntimeout period (t\nWD), reset is asserted. The microprocessor is \nrequired to toggle the WDI pin to avoid being reset. Failure of \nthe microprocessor to toggle WDI within the timeout period, therefore, indicates a code execution error, and the reset pulse generated restarts the microprocessor in a known state. \nAs well as logic transitions on WDI, the watchdog timer is also \ncleared by a reset assertion due to an undervoltage condition on \nV\nCC or due to MR being pulled low. When reset is asserted, the \nwatchdog timer is cleared and does not begin counting again until \nreset deasserts. The watchdog timer can be disabled by leaving \nWDI floating or by three-stating the WDI driver. \nVCC\n1V\nVCC\n0V\nVCC\n0VVTH\n0VVCC\nWDIRESET tRP tRP tWD\n04533-022 \nFigure 17. When driven high, WDI can draw as much as 160 μA. Pulsing WDI low-to-high-to-low at a low duty cycle \nreduces the effect of the large input current. When WDI is \nunconnected, a window comparator disconnects the watchdog timer from the reset output circuitry so that reset is not asserted when the watchdog timer times out. \nNEGATIVE GOING V CC TRANSIENTS \nTo avoid unnecessary resets caused by fast power supply transients, the ADM6316/ADM6318/ADM6319/ADM6320/ADM6321/ ADM6322 are equipped with glitch rejection circuitry. The typical performance characteristic in Figure 13 plots V\nCC transient du-\nration vs. the transient magnitude. The curves show combinations of transient magnitude and duration for which a reset is not generated for 4.63 V and 2.93 V reset threshold parts. For example, with the 2.93 V threshold, a transient that goes 100 mV below the threshold and lasts 8 μs typically does not cause a reset, but \nif the transient is any larger in magnitude or duration, a reset is \ngenerated. An optional 0.1 μF bypass capacitor mounted close to V\nCC provides additional glitch rejection. \nENSURING RESET VALID TO V CC = 0 V \nBoth active low and active high reset outputs are guaranteed to be valid for V\nCC as low as 1 V . For an active low reset output, a resistor \nconnected between RESET  and ground pulls the output low when \nit is unable to sink current. For the active high case, a resistor \nconnected between RESET and V CC pulls the output high when it \nis unable to source current. Ensuring Reset Valid to V CC = 0 V WATCHDOG SOFTWARE CONSIDERATIONS \nIn implementing the microprocessor’s watchdog strobe code, \nquickly switching WDI low to high and then high to low (mini-mizing WDI high time) is desirable for current consumption reasons. However, a more effective way of using the watchdog \nfunction can be considered. \nA low-to-high-to-low WDI pulse within a given subroutine \nprevents the watchdog from timing out. However, if the sub-\nroutine becomes stuck in an infinite loop, the watchdog cannot detect this because the subroutine continues to toggle WDI. A more effective coding scheme for detecting this error involves using a slightly longer watchdog timeout. In the program that calls the subroutine, WDI is set high. The subroutine sets WDI \nlow when it is called. If the program executes without error, WDI \nis toggled high and low with every loop of the program. If the subroutine enters an infinite loop, WDI is kept low, the watchdog times out, and the microprocessor is reset (see Figure 19). \nSTART\nSET WDI\nHIGH\nPROGRAM\nCODE\nSUBROUTINE\nSET WDI\nLOW\nRETURNINFINITE LOOP:\nWATCHDOG\nTIMES OUTRESET\n04533-021 \nFigure 19. ### Component Summary: ADM6320CX29ARJZ-R7

**Key Characteristics and Specifications:**

- **Voltage Ratings:**
  - Operating Voltage Range: 1 V to 5.5 V
  - Reset Threshold Voltage: 2.93 V (specific to this variant)

- **Current Ratings:**
  - Supply Current: 5 μA (typical at VCC = 3.6 V)
  - Reset Output Current: 20 mA (maximum)

- **Power Consumption:**
  - Low power consumption: 5 μA (typical)

- **Operating Temperature Range:**
  - -40°C to +85°C

- **Package Type:**
  - 5-lead SOT-23

- **Special Features or Notes:**
  - 26 reset threshold options (2.5 V to 5 V in 100 mV increments)
  - 4 reset timeout options: 1 ms, 20 ms, 140 ms, and 1120 ms (minimum)
  - 4 watchdog timeout options: 6.3 ms, 102 ms, 1600 ms, and 25.6 sec (typical)
  - Manual reset input
  - Open-drain active low reset output
  - Guaranteed reset output valid to VCC = 1 V
  - Power supply glitch immunity

- **Moisture Sensitive Level (JEDEC J-STD-020E):**
  - Level 1 (not moisture sensitive)

**Description of the Component:**
The ADM6320 is a supervisory circuit designed to monitor power supply voltage levels and ensure the integrity of code execution in microprocessor-based systems. It provides power-on reset signals and includes an on-chip watchdog timer that resets the microprocessor if it fails to respond within a specified timeout period. The ADM6320 features an open-drain reset output and a manual reset input, allowing for external reset capabilities.

**Typical Applications:**
The ADM6320 is commonly used in:
- Microprocessor systems
- Computers
- Controllers
- Intelligent instruments
- Portable equipment

These applications benefit from the ADM6320's ability to prevent code execution errors during power fluctuations and to ensure reliable operation of microprocessor systems by monitoring voltage levels and providing reset signals when necessary.
